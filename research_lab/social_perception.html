Overview

As an undergraduate research assistant in UCLA's Social Vision and Multimodal Perception Lab, I supported dissertation research on first impressions and multimodal social perception — examining how faces, bodies, clothing, and voices converge to shape rapid judgments of social status, competence, and personality. My role extended across nearly the entire research pipeline: from study preparation and literature review to participant-facing execution, stimulus production, and post-processing.

The research centers on how people form consequential social judgments within milliseconds of encountering another person — and what information, across multiple sensory channels, drives those assessments.

Responsibilities

  • Built the study's experimental infrastructure by calibrating camera, lighting, backdrop, and software workflows to ensure standardization across all participant sessions.
  • Ran participant sessions end-to-end: intake, informed consent, surveys, structured vocal recordings, and guided photography across multiple outfit conditions and emotional expressions.
  • Monitored audio quality in real time, maintained visual consistency across shots, and directed participants with technical precision while preserving participant comfort and data integrity.
  • Managed file transfers, archiving, and post-production using Adobe Audition, Lightroom Classic, and Photoshop — including white balance correction with ColorChecker calibration and green-screen artifact removal.
  • Organized and labeled large volumes of image and audio data, uploading finalized files into the lab's centralized database for downstream analysis.
  • Conducted literature review, coded behavioral data, organized experimental stimuli, and helped refine the study's design around identity-based cues and first impression formation.

Impact

This role was both technically demanding and intellectually substantive — requiring fluency in production workflows alongside engagement with the theoretical questions driving the research. Working across the full pipeline of a doctoral-level study gave me direct insight into what rigorous empirical social psychology looks like from the ground up, and deepened my understanding of how perception, judgment, and identity intersect in everyday social life.
