Overview

This research explores yoga as a somatic practice — examining how sustained attentional observation of bodily sensation facilitates recovery from physical strain, emotional stress, and the cumulative wear of performance training. Drawing on somatic theory, yoga philosophy, and phenomenological accounts of practice, the paper investigates what it means to inhabit the body with receptive awareness rather than instrumental control.

The study combines theoretical analysis with first-person somatic observation, tracing how yoga's emphasis on breath, alignment, and internal sensing creates conditions for physical and psychological restoration that purely mechanical exercise regimes cannot replicate.

Key Findings

  • Somatic attention as recovery mechanism: how directing conscious awareness to bodily sensation — rather than outcome or performance — activates restorative physiological and psychological processes.
  • Breath as anchor: the central role of pranayama in regulating the autonomic nervous system, creating the conditions for recovery from both physical injury and emotional dysregulation.
  • Yoga vs. rehabilitation: how yoga's holistic, attention-based approach differs fundamentally from physical therapy's biomechanical framework — and what each misses without the other.
  • The observing body: phenomenological accounts of what practitioners describe as "listening to the body" — and how this listening differs from pain management or performance monitoring.
  • Cultural context: how yoga's translation from South Asian spiritual practice to Western wellness product has altered its somatic and recuperative functions — what is lost, what is preserved, and what new possibilities emerge.

Significance

This research contributes to scholarship on somatic practices as therapeutic and recuperative modalities — challenging purely biomechanical approaches to recovery and centering the practitioner's subjective experience. It has particular relevance for dancers, athletes, and others whose practices place sustained physical demands on the body, offering somatic observation as a complement to conventional recovery protocols.
