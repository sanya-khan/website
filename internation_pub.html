Health-Related Quality of Life in Pediatric Autoinflammatory Disease — India's Pioneering HRQoL Study Using KIDSCREEN-52

Overview

This study represents India's first application of the KIDSCREEN-52 instrument to assess health-related quality of life in children with autoinflammatory diseases — addressing a critical gap in pediatric rare disease research where no prior HRQoL data existed for this population in India.

The work was conducted at SRCC Children's Hospital, Mumbai, and presented as a peer-reviewed poster at the 13th ISSAID International Conference in Paris in 2025. A full manuscript is currently in preparation for international journal submission.

Contributions

  • Conducted bilingual interviews with 20+ caregivers of pediatric patients with autoinflammatory diseases, gathering psychosocial data across 10 KIDSCREEN-52 domains including physical well-being, psychological health, social support, and school environment.
  • Calculated and synthesized quality-of-life scores for each participant, identifying disparities in healthcare access and well-being across the study population.
  • Generated pilot trend statistics from the HRQoL data to identify patterns and inform future research directions in pediatric autoinflammatory disease in low-resource settings.
  • Contributed to poster design and presentation for the 13th ISSAID International Conference in Paris — the study's first international dissemination.
  • Supporting manuscript preparation for submission to an international peer-reviewed journal, building on the pilot data gathered at SRCC.

Significance

Autoinflammatory diseases are rare and frequently misdiagnosed, particularly in low- and middle-income countries where specialist access is limited. By applying a validated, multi-domain HRQoL instrument to this population for the first time in India, this study generates foundational data that can guide both clinical practice and health policy for a historically underserved pediatric group.

The bilingual interview methodology — conducted in English and Hindi — was essential for capturing caregivers' experiences accurately and ensured the study could reach a representative sample across Mumbai's diverse patient population. The findings contribute to the global evidence base for pediatric autoinflammatory disease and lay the groundwork for a larger, multi-center HRQoL study across India.
